Project Overview

Date: September 2020 to December 2020

Role: UX Researcher, UX/UI Designer

Teammate: An Huynh (UX Researcher, UX/UI Designer)

Tools: Figma

Background

During the pandemic, customers picking up at a local restaurant is pretty common in Domino’s Pizza. It would help not only reduce contact between people, but also save some money and time from shipping. Nevertheless, customers and Domino’s Pizza’s employees still have to directly communicate with each other during the confirmation process, which might cause anxiety to many people.

Research Methods

We chose interviews as the research method, because we would like to talk in depth with users, and have open ended questions. Therefore, we would have more qualitative information, and there would be more to analyze.

Also, our users would like to talk with us by calling through phones, or using Zoom. For our plan, we were going to interview about 5 to 7 people for our project. Additionally, the participants were relevant to our project because they often ate pizza, and would like to use an app to save their time, and be safer during the pandemic.

Research Findings

1. Most people we interviewed including domino’s employee said that customers have to wait longer than their ideal time during the pandemic.

2. Even though they felt okay most of the time, they would like to have less waiting time

3. Some people might get irritated sometimes, especially when they were starving.

4. The employee also pointed out that they sometimes did not have enough space for cooked-pizzas (keep warm before pick-up) because of stacked orders.

5. Most participants were okay with directly talking to employees, because they wore their mask. However, they preferred to reduce contact with employees as much as possible in order to be safer.

6. Additionally, a few participants mentioned that it would be great to reduce the time wearing a mask, since they were not comfortable with wearing it. Therefore, they preferred the “in car” option for picking up their food. With the “in car” option, they could stay in their car and did not have to wear a mask unless it was necessary. Also, they did not have to stand outside, especially if there were many other people also waiting for their food.

Problem Statement

People who prefer to pick up their pizzas usually have to wait longer than their ideal time, and they might get frustrated if they wait too long, or they are starving.

In order to make the process of picking up pizzas safer and more efficient, Domino’s Pizza needs a way to have better communication between its customers and its employees.

How Might We Statements

How might we help customers spend less time on waiting to save their time?

How might we help employees have an easier time noticing when customers come to pick up?

How might we help employees not feel frustrated because there are too many customers tapping the “I’m Here” button at the same time?

Personas

Persona 1: Helen

Age: 28

A software engineer working remotely at home

She wants to have her pizza quickly, so that she can pick up the pizza from the store that is close to her house. Also, she prefers carside carryout to reduce contact in the pandemic.

Sometimes she has to wait for a bit too long, that she might get anxious when she still has a lot of tasks to do for her job.

Persona 2: Adam

Age: 23

An employee working at a pizza restaurant

Adam does not want his customers to wait for a long line to carry out their pizza.

However, he notices that many times customers have to wait in a long line, and they get frustrated. Especially when some people feel uncomfortable that they have to wear a mask.

Competitive Audit

Delivery Yes Yes Yes

Recognize Customers No Yes No

For Domino’s Pizza mobile application, there is no feature that can recognize customers who are ready to pick up. Thus, this gives frustration to both customers and employees. Since there are many users tapping the new feature at the same time, employees have to spend more time to recognize the identity of users. Because of that, users have to wait for an extra time before picking up their pizza. Additionally, it might be safer if the time of interacting between customers and employees is reduced.

UI Sketches

Sketch 1

The first sketch offers a new feature which helps customers to save more time and employees to identify customers easier. Instead of directly asking customer’s or vehicle’s information, this flow allows employees to have enough information to know who is in front of the door and what their order is. For users, this flow reduces contacting with other people under the pandemic which is very important for user’s health.

Sketch 2

Similar to the first sketch, the second one also reduces contacting with other people under the pandemic, which is very important for user’s health, by having enough information before giving the food instead of directly asking customers. However, there are a few differences in the process. Instead of showing codes in the first one, users can enter their information about themselves, including their appearance for picking up in person. While the process for “In Car” has the same UX flow, it is actually a little bit different about details in Sketches section.

Low Fidelity Prototypes

Prototype 1

For this prototype, it has two options to pick up, which are “In Person” and “Contactless” (or “In Car”). For “In Person”, it has a number on the app that helps employees recognize which food they should give to the particular customer. When customers arrive, they can show their numbers in front of the restaurant to let employees know. For “Contactless”, it has one dropdown option to choose for the type of vehicle (motorcycle, sedan, suv, truck, etc.), and one input field for license plate number. With that information of the vehicle, it helps employees have an easier time to recognize which vehicle customers are in, in order to quickly give the food to them.

Prototype 2

Similarly, Prototype 2 also has the same two options to pick up. However, there are a few differences in details of what information customers can provide to help employees. For “In Person”, it asks for the description of customers’ appearance, including “Gender/Race”, “Shirt Color”, and “Pant Color”. Thus, employees can recognize different customers more easily with different details about each customer. For “In Car”, it asks for the “Make/Model”, and “Vehicle Color”. Similar to Prototype 1, “In Car” option helps employees have an easier time to recognize which vehicle customers are in, in order to quickly give the food to them.

Low Fidelity User Testing

What users liked most about Prototype 1 was it was more simple in “In Person” option, since it only showed the number to confirm with the employees. While, what they liked most about Prototype 2 was they did not have to type license plate numbers, which many people did not remember them, and had to go out of the vehicle and check for the numbers.

For Prototype 1, the only confusion was the confirmation number of the “In Person” option, since it was not descriptive enough in order to guide customers what to do with it. While, there was no struggling or confusion when they used Prototype 2.

Overall, they preferred to use Prototype 1, since it was more simple to use, and had less details to fill out.

For the “In Person” option, they preferred Prototype 1, because they did not like to fill out details about their appearance. Additionally, they mentioned that asking about “Gender/Race” might be sensitive to many people, which may lead to uncomfortable situations.

For the “In Car” option, they did not prefer either prototype. Nevertheless, one of the participants suggested having mix options from both prototypes for the “In Car” option including “Type of Vehicle”, “Make/Model”, and “Vehicle Color”.

High Fidelity Prototype

The high-fidelity prototype actually implements the mix of both of our original UX Flows, since we got feedback from the users that they prefer some features of the first flow, while they also prefer some features of the second flow. Therefore, we did not have to change our flow technically, even though it took a bit to select the features from both flows to combine into one high fidelity prototype.

What we learned from the iteration?

• We learned that it was really good to have feedback from users, that we could learn more from different perspectives.

• We also realized that sometimes there were details that we did not recognize during our design process, and the users actually helped us a lot in spotting the details that we needed to improve for our design.

Before-and-After Stories

After feedback, we decided to use the alternative screen for “Vehicle Carryout”. For the original screen, when users typed information in “Make / Model / Brand”, the word “Make / Model / Brand” would disappear. If users forgot what information they needed to type in, they had to delete everything and started over again. Therefore, putting the word “Make / Model / Brand” above the text input field instead of putting it inside would help users be able to avoid that situation.

Next Steps

If we were to continue this project, we would like to have more interviews with people who eat pizzas, in order to gather more insights. From that, we would be able to look at, and see which aspects that we could improve further.

Final Reflection

It was pretty interesting that my teammate and I learned how to address when things did not go as planned. It actually helped us improve problem-solving skills, and how to communicate with other people effectively when going through challenges.
